A Novel Data Integration Method Identifies Inflamed Patients from Single-Cell RNAseq Data and Differentiates Cell Type Specific Markers of T cell Infiltration

The discovery of immune checkpoint-based medicines has been a significant step in cancer treatment, with a subset of patients displaying remarkable and long-lasting responses across a wide range of tumor types. Pre-existing T cell infiltration in the tumor immunological microenvironment is a prognostic biomarker for immunotherapy response (TIME).

Using metagenes/deconvolution techniques, bulk transcriptomics-based approaches may coarsely and indirectly measure the degree of T-cell infiltration, and so discriminate inflamed and immunologically chilled tumors. However, in the TIME, these bulk approaches are unable to distinguish biomarkers of particular cell types.

Recently, assays based on single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNAseq) have been employed to characterize a granular account of the TIME. To the best of our knowledge, no approach exists for identifying patients with T-cell inflamed TIME utilizing both scRNAseq and bulk transcriptomics data. We present the 'Identifying Biological Relationships in Dark Matter of Genomics Entities' (I-BRIDGE) technique, which combines TCGA bulk RNA-seq data with the malignant/epithelial component of scRNAseq data to identify patients with T-cell inflamed TIME.

We present the indicators of inflamed morphologies in malignant cells, myeloid cells, and fibroblasts using this technique. Thus, Type I and Type II interferon pathways are identified as prominent signals, particularly in malignant and myeloid cells. In contrast, immune cell metagenes have been identified as the primary indicators of inflammatory tumors in large datasets.These cell-type specific indicators may contain causal targetable genes that, when regulated, can turn a cool TIME into an inflammatory TIME.

I-BRIDGE may be used on in vitro grown cancer cell lines to identify cell lines that are likely to be adapted from inflamed/cold patient tumors.

Understanding vector ecology, arbovirus infection rates and insecticide resistance to optimize mosquito control

TEAM ARBO uses the CDC's Southeastern Center of Excellence in Vector Borne Diseases (SEVBD) program's unique research environment to combine epidemiology, entomology, and molecular biology techniques to address acute and essential state-wide public health issues. We place a premium on fostering a friendly and collaborative work environment, as well as striving for excellence in both scientific communication and research. We collaborate to investigate the epidemiology and molecular biology of arthropod-borne viruses, with a particular focus on Zika and dengue fever. The Chikungunya and Rift Valley Fever viruses will be included in future research.

Aim 1: Investigate the ecology of Aedes vectors and arbovirus infection rates in Miami-Dade County metropolitan areas

Aim 2: Focus on Highly Attractive Biological Insecticide Traps (HABITraps)

Aim 3: Power vector control tools with strategies to circumvent insecticide resistance

Aim 4: Investigation of naturally occurring Wolbachia infections in Aedes aegypti populations in Florida

Understanding the Structure and function of the pol II transcription machinery

The Pre-Initiation Complex (PIC), which is made up of Mediator, TFIIA, TFIIB, TFIID, TFIIE, TFIIF, TFIIH, and RNA polymerase II (pol II), regulates the expression of all protein-coding and most non-coding RNA genes in the human genome. It is roughly 4.0 MDa in size. Mediator and TFIID, both around 1.4 MDa in size, are important assemblies inside the PIC that work to modulate PIC function in ways that are just now becoming clear. Mediator and TFIID structural properties (conformationally dynamic, extended shape) combined with their huge size (e.g., human Mediator roughly 320 x 180 x 160 ) give a vast surface for interaction with other proteins or nucleic acids.

TFIIH is likewise a big and structurally dynamic protein complex (10 subunits, 0.5MDa). TFIIH, like Mediator and TFIID, is essential for pol II transcription across the genome. Notably, Mediator, TFIID, and TFIIH appear to work together inside the PIC; nevertheless, the molecular mechanisms underlying their coordinated or synergistic action are unknown.

We apply a variety of biochemical, cellular, and biophysical techniques to better understand how Mediator, TFIID, and TFIIH collaborate with other PIC components to control pol II transcription start, promoter-proximal pausing, and elongation.
